## The through-line

Read top to bottom, the field poses one question the Thousand Brains programme has to answer: **is there a single repeated cortical unit running a common algorithm?** Mountcastle said yes; the column skeptics (Horton & Adams; Haueis) say the *anatomical* column is a developmental byproduct; the canonical-microcircuit and dendritic-computation work says the repeated motif is real but far richer than a point-neuron column; predictive coding and the grid-cell/reference-frame thread give candidate *algorithms* for that unit. Your thesis lives exactly in this gap — it treats the unit as real-but-not-hand-designable and proposes to **grow** it.

## The cortical column — organizing metaphor and its skeptics

The cortical column has been neocortex's dominant organizing metaphor since Mountcastle's 1957 finding that neurons in a vertical electrode penetration share modality and receptive-field properties; Hubel & Wiesel's orientation and ocular-dominance columns, plus barrels and cytochrome-oxidase blobs, seemed to confirm a repeating modular unit, and Mountcastle (1978, 1997) elevated it to a universal cortical algorithm. But a strong modern skeptical camp — crystallized by Horton & Adams (2005) "a structure without a function," and reinforced by da Costa & Martin (2010) and Rockland (2010) — argues that columns exist anatomically in some regions and species but vary so much (present in some species/individuals, absent in others; no visual faculty tracks their presence) that there is no single canonical function; ocular-dominance columns in particular look like developmental byproducts ("spandrels"). The consensus that has settled is nuanced: real, locally meaningful periodic structures exist, but "the column" is not a proven universal computational building block — a tension that matters directly for any theory (Thousand Brains) that treats a uniform, repeated column as the atom of intelligence.

**What is established.**

SETTLED HISTORICAL / EMPIRICAL FACTS:

- Vertical (radial) functional organization is real in primary sensory cortices. Mountcastle (1955, 1957) recording in cat S1 found that all cells in a vertical penetration respond to one submodality (skin vs deep), switching abruptly at column boundaries (~<500 µm wide) — the founding "quantal/discrete" columnar observation. Powell & Mountcastle (1959) added the cytoarchitectural correlate.

- Hubel & Wiesel independently found in cat (1962, 1963) and monkey (1968, 1974, 1977 Ferrier Lecture) striate cortex: (a) ORIENTATION COLUMNS — vertically aligned cells share preferred orientation, shifting ~10°/50 µm along tangential tracks; (b) OCULAR DOMINANCE COLUMNS (1969) — eye-preference stripes, ~500 µm, visualizable by transneuronal [³H]proline, reduced-silver stain, and cytochrome-oxidase after monocular deprivation. Their "ice-cube/hypercolumn" model packaged a full set of orientations + 2 eyes into a ~1 mm² module.

- Other periodic structures confirmed the modular picture: whisker BARRELS in rodent layer 4 (Woolsey & Van der Loos 1970) — the cleanest anatomically discrete column, one per whisker; CYTOCHROME-OXIDASE BLOBS/patches in V1 (Horton & Hubel 1981; Horton 1984) and stripes in V2 (Tootell; Sincich & Horton 2002); color/direction columns in extrastriate and IT cortex.

- MINICOLUMN: a narrow radial chain of ~80–100 neurons (2.5× more in striate cortex) spanning layers II–VI, ~30–50 µm diameter, tied to the developmental radial glial migration column. Mountcastle (1997) redefined the minicolumn as "the basic unit of the mature neocortex," with the classical "column" being a functional grouping of many minicolumns bound by short horizontal connections. Buxhoeveden & Casanova (2002, Brain) reviewed it as a real but heterogeneous module (varies across areas/species, implicated in autism/schizophrenia pathology).

- DEVELOPMENTAL origin: Rakic's radial-unit / protomap hypotheses (1988, 1995) — cortex is built as an array of ontogenetic radial columns from ventricular-zone proliferative units migrating along radial glia; cortical expansion = adding radial units. This gives columns a real embryological substrate independent of adult function (Horton & Adams note Nissl "ontogenetic columns" with "no known functional relationship to columns in the adult cortex").

- Rockel, Hiorns & Powell (1980) claimed a near-constant number of neurons (~110, ~260 in V1) under any point of cortex across areas and species — long used as evidence for a uniform minicolumnar unit; this "basic uniformity" claim has since been substantially challenged/qualified (V1 and area/species exceptions).

WHAT THE SKEPTICS ESTABLISHED (Horton & Adams 2005, read directly):
- The term "column" has been stretched from Mountcastle's discrete modality block to ANY vertical cluster sharing a receptive-field attribute, and to the periodic termination of projections (blobs, stripes) — losing a common definition.
- Ocular-dominance columns are PRESENT in cat, ferret, mink, and >12 primate species but ABSENT in mouse, rat, squirrel, rabbit, possum, sheep, goat; the only correlate of presence is being a predator, and no visual faculty (including stereopsis) tracks their presence/absence.
- The "fatal blow": ODC expression is HIGHLY VARIABLE within a species and even within one individual's V1. Adams & Horton (2003) found a full spectrum in normal squirrel monkeys — from well-developed macaque-like columns to rudimentary or essentially absent (~30% of animals essentially lack them); columns can be present in only part of V1. Twofold variation in ODC periodicity among normal macaques (Horton & Hocking 1996) and cats (Rathjen 2002). A structure so variable is hard to assign an indispensable function.
- Orientation columns are also not obligatory: absent/weak in mice, rats, hamsters, rabbits — yet the squirrel, which has excellent acuity and a cone-rich retina, has NO orientation columns (Van Hooser et al. 2005) while having sharply tuned cells, refuting "acuity requires columns."
- Conclusion: columns are likely a developmental/evolutionary BYPRODUCT — Horton & Adams invoke Gould's "SPANDREL" (Gould 1997) and retinal-wave/activity-dependent self-organization as the cause, not a computational requirement. "Although the column is an attractive concept, it has failed as a unifying principle for understanding cortical function."

**Open questions / controversies.**

- REAL UNIT vs EPIPHENOMENON — the core dispute: Is the column a functional computational unit or a developmental/vascular/wiring byproduct? Horton & Adams, da Costa & Martin lean epiphenomenon (spandrel); Mountcastle/Buxhoeveden/Hawkins camp treats it as a genuine unit. Rockland and most anatomists now hold an intermediate 'it depends on which column, region, species' position.
- DEFINITION PROBLEM: 'column' conflates at least four things — modality/feature columns (function), minicolumns (radial developmental chains), hypercolumns (a full stimulus set), and periodic projection compartments (blobs/stripes/barrels). Whether these share anything beyond verticality is unresolved; Haueis (2021) argues the concept has fragmented into a 'patchwork' warranting 'conceptual retirement.'
- Does the Rockel–Hiorns–Powell constant-neuron-number claim survive? Modern stereology finds systematic variation (notably V1, rodents), weakening the uniform-minicolumn premise — but how much uniformity remains is still argued.
- If ocular-dominance columns have no function, WHY are they so metabolically/anatomically elaborate and conserved in predators? Candidate answers (wiring-length minimization, activity-dependent self-organization from retinal waves, a neutral spandrel) are not decisively resolved.
- Do barrels (clear function: one whisker) generalize, or are they the exception that proves feature columns are region-specific rather than a universal plan?
- Species WITHOUT columns (rodents, squirrel) perform the same computations — so is the columnar map a substrate for computation or merely one possible physical layout of it? (Directly bears on whether repetition of a canonical module is necessary.)
- Rodent V1 'salt-and-pepper' orientation maps vs carnivore/primate ordered maps: same circuit, different geometry — what determines which, and does it change the computation?

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

DIRECTLY LOAD-BEARING and double-edged for a Thousand Brains / grow-the-architecture thesis.

The Thousand Brains Theory (Hawkins, Lewis, Klukas, Purdy & Ahmad 2019, 'A Framework for Intelligence and Cortical Function Based on Grid Cells in the Neocortex,' Front. Neural Circuits 12:121; and the 2021 book) is EXPLICITLY built on Mountcastle's strongest claim: the neocortex is ~150k near-identical cortical columns, each running the SAME sensorimotor algorithm — learning object models in grid-cell/reference-frame coordinates — with intelligence emerging from voting across many such repeated modules. A 'grow the architecture by repeating a canonical module' thesis depends on that uniformity/repetition being real.

The skeptical literature is the sharpest challenge to that foundation and must be engaged head-on:
1. Horton & Adams (2005) and da Costa & Martin (2010) show the ANATOMICAL column is not a discrete, universally present, canonically bounded structure — so 'the column' TBT builds on is a FUNCTIONAL posit, not a clean physical brick. TBT should be defended at the level of a functional patch running a sensorimotor loop, not the ocular-dominance-column-style structure the skeptics dismantle; conflating them invites the 'spandrel' objection.
2. The variability evidence (ODCs present/absent across species and even within one animal; rodents/squirrel compute vision fine without ordered columns) cuts BOTH ways: it undercuts 'columns are essential,' but it also SUPPORTS TBT's abstraction — if the same computation runs whether or not columnar geometry is expressed, then the computational module (reference-frame modeling) is what repeats, and the visible column map is incidental layout. That is arguably a friendlier reading for a repetition-based architecture than the classical Hubel–Wiesel one.
3. The MINICOLUMN survives the critique better than the macrocolumn and is the better anchor for a 'repeat-the-module' story. See the recent bridge to ML: 'The Generalist Brain Module: Module Repetition in Neural Networks in Light of the Minicolumn Hypothesis' (arXiv 2507.12473, 2025) — module repetition (weight sharing / a single reused block scaled up) as the engineering analog of minicolumnar repetition. A grow-the-architecture argument is strongest framed as 'a repeated GENERALIST module' (minicolumn/functional column) rather than 'a repeated Mountcastle column.'
4. Honest framing for the thesis: Mountcastle's uniform-algorithm conjecture is an inspiring but UNPROVEN premise; Rockland's 'columns are not obligatory' and Haueis's 'conceptual retirement' mean the burden is on the model to show the repeated unit is functional, not anatomical. The defensible position: adopt Mountcastle's common-cortical-algorithm intuition and the minicolumn's repetition, but DECOUPLE it from the discredited claim that the ocular-dominance-style column is a necessary computational unit.

## The canonical cortical microcircuit and laminar organization

The neocortex is a ~2-4 mm thick sheet of six horizontal layers whose local excitatory-inhibitory wiring looks broadly similar wherever it is measured, motivating the "canonical microcircuit" idea: a single connectivity motif — thalamic/feedforward input arriving mainly in layer 4, ascending to superficial L2/3, then descending to output layers L5/L6 — that is repeated across cortical areas and species. Quantitative anatomy (Binzegger, Douglas & Martin) and modern cell-type taxonomies (Harris & Shepherd) confirm a strongly conserved skeleton but show it is dominated by dense recurrent intralaminar excitation, not the tidy L4-to-L2/3-to-L5 feedforward chain of textbook diagrams. Whether there is one true canonical circuit remains contested: the field has shifted from "identical repeated circuit" toward "a conserved template (serial homology / shared hodology) with area- and species-specific variations," and skeptics argue there are many reusable circuit motifs rather than one.

**What is established.**

SIX-LAYER LAMINAR PLAN. Neocortex has six cytoarchitectonic layers (L1 molecular/cell-sparse; L2/3 supragranular pyramidal; L4 granular with spiny stellate/star pyramid cells; L5 infragranular with large pyramids incl. thick-tufted; L6 with pyramids and modified cells). Layer thickness and L4 development vary by area: primary sensory areas are "granular/koniocortex" (thick, dense L4), whereas motor and many frontal/limbic areas are "agranular/dysgranular" (reduced or absent L4). This L4 gradient (eulaminate to agranular) is the main systematic laminar variation across the sheet.

THE CANONICAL MICROCIRCUIT (Douglas & Martin). Origin: Douglas, Martin & Whitteridge (1989, Neural Computation) coined "canonical microcircuit" from intracellular recordings in cat V1; Douglas & Martin (1991, J Physiol) showed thalamic (electrical) stimulation triggers a stereotyped excitation-then-inhibition sequence in every neuron, modeled by a minimal circuit of superficial + deep pyramidal populations sharing a common inhibitory pool, all receiving thalamic drive. Core information-flow claim (idealized): thalamic input -> L4 -> L2/3 (superficial) -> L5/L6 (deep output) -> back to thalamus (L6) and out of cortex (L5). Key mechanistic insight: recurrent intracortical excitation amplifies weak thalamic input, and excitation and inhibition are inseparable, balanced, and dynamically coupled ("the thalamic input is small; the cortex mostly talks to itself").

QUANTITATIVE CONNECTIVITY (Binzegger, Douglas & Martin 2004, J Neurosci; 3D reconstruction of 39 HRP-filled cat V1 neurons). Thalamic afferents supply only ~5-10% of excitatory synapses even onto their main L4 target; the great majority of synapses onto L4 spiny stellates are cortical (large fractions from L6 pyramids and from other L4 cells). Local recurrent/intralaminar connections dominate: within-layer self-innervation accounts for a large share (~1/3) of excitatory synapses, and the classic feedforward L4->L2/3->L5->L6 loop is only ~20% of excitatory synapses between excitatory neurons. Motif: a few "strong" but very many "weak" projections. This is the empirical anchor for "cortex is mostly recurrent."

LAYER-SPECIFIC INPUT/OUTPUT. Input: driving (feedforward) thalamic input targets L4 (and L6, and L5B/border); L1 receives feedback and higher-order/matrix thalamic axons plus neuromodulatory/top-down input onto apical dendrites. Output: L5 (esp. thick-tufted L5B pyramidal-tract cells) is the major subcerebral/long-range output to brainstem, spinal cord, tectum, striatum; L6 is the corticothalamic feedback output (and modulates thalamus); L2/3 provides the main cortico-cortical (associational and feedforward-to-higher-area) output. Superficial layers = intracortical communication; deep layers = subcortical broadcasting + feedback.

FEEDFORWARD vs FEEDBACK LAMINAR RULES (Felleman & Van Essen 1991, Cerebral Cortex; Rockland & Pandya). In primates, feedforward projections originate mainly in supragranular L2/3 and terminate in L4 of the higher area; feedback projections originate in infragranular layers (L5/6) and terminate outside L4, especially in L1 (and L6), avoiding L4. These laminar-origin/termination rules let the ~30+ visual areas be ordered into an anatomical hierarchy. In rodents the laminar segregation is weaker; the most reliable feature is that feedback avoids L4.

CELL-TYPE TAXONOMY (Harris & Shepherd 2015, Nat Neurosci). Excitatory (glutamatergic) projection neurons fall into three conserved classes defined by long-range axonal target: IT (intratelencephalic, in L2-6, project only within telencephalon incl. contralateral cortex via corpus callosum and striatum); PT (pyramidal-tract / SPN, thick-tufted L5B, to brainstem/spinal cord/midbrain/subcerebral); CT (corticothalamic, L6, to ipsilateral thalamus). Inhibitory (GABAergic) interneurons fall into three largely non-overlapping genetic classes: Pvalb (parvalbumin: fast-spiking basket + chandelier, perisomatic/axo-axonic inhibition), Sst (somatostatin: Martinotti, dendrite-targeting, inhibit pyramidal apical dendrites and PV cells), and Htr3a/5HT3aR (incl. VIP cells that disinhibit by inhibiting other interneurons). A directional local excitatory flow exists: L4 is upstream (projects to L2/3 and L5, receives little excitatory feedback); L2/3 projects down to L5A/B; PT cells receive from many IT layers but give little back locally.

TRANSCRIPTOMIC CENSUS (Tasic et al. 2018, Nature; BICCN). Single-cell RNA-seq across distant mouse areas (V1 and ALM) found ~100+ transcriptomic types: nearly all GABAergic types are shared across areas, whereas many glutamatergic types are area-specific — a molecular restatement of "conserved inhibitory framework, area-tuned excitatory content." Interneuron classes map cleanly onto the Pvalb/Sst/Vip developmental-genetic scheme.

UNIFORMITY / COLUMNS. Mountcastle's columnar-organization hypothesis (radial minicolumn as elementary module; 1957, 1978, 1997 Brain) provides the structural rationale for a repeated circuit. The vertical inter-laminar loop plus tangential (horizontal) intralaminar connectivity is "evident wherever it has been sought" (Douglas & Martin 2004, Annu Rev Neurosci), which is the strongest statement of cortical uniformity.

**Open questions / controversies.**

1) DOES ONE CANONICAL CIRCUIT EVEN EXIST? ~40 years on there is no consensus that a single anatomical+functional canonical circuit exists (Marcus/Marblestone/Dean 2014); the pendulum has swung to "conserved template + variations." How much variation is tolerable before 'canonical' loses meaning is unresolved. 2) GRANULAR vs AGRANULAR. The classic circuit is built on granular sensory cortex; agranular motor/frontal/limbic cortex lacks a clear L4, so a truly universal motif must explain input routing without a canonical L4 (work 'Towards a canonical agranular cortical microcircuit', Beul & Hilgetag). 3) WHAT IS THE INVARIANT — lamination, columns, or hodology? Harris & Shepherd argue the conserved unit is the connectivity pattern among genetically-defined cell classes, not layers per se; the reality/definition/heterogeneity of the cortical column and minicolumn is itself still debated. 4) FUNCTION. We still lack an agreed computational function for the circuit — competing accounts include predictive coding (Bastos/Friston), normalization/gain control, recurrent amplification/attractor dynamics, and Bayesian inference; the structure-function mapping is unsettled. 5) SPECIES SCALING. How rodent circuits (weak laminar FF/FB segregation) generalize to primate/human cortex (sharp laminar hierarchy, expanded L2/3, more interneuron diversity) is open. 6) INTERNEURON ENUMERATION. The exact number and functional roles of GABAergic subtypes, and whether transcriptomic types equal functional types, is an active frontier. 7) LONG-RANGE vs LOCAL WEIGHTING. Given that ~90-95% of L4 synapses are cortical, how the numerically tiny but functionally 'driving' thalamic input controls the recurrent network remains a core puzzle.

This sub-field is the empirical bedrock for the Thousand Brains functional cortical model. (1) The uniformity/repeated-circuit claim (Mountcastle's columnar principle; Douglas & Martin's 'evident wherever sought') is exactly the premise the Thousand Brains Theory needs: a single cortical-column algorithm replicated ~150,000 times, each column a near-complete sensorimotor modeling system. A grow-the-architecture thesis (build one canonical module, tile it) rests on how canonical the circuit truly is — so the live debate (Marcus/Marblestone/Dean skepticism; Harris & Shepherd's 'variations') directly bounds how literal the copy-paste can be. TBP should adopt the 'conserved template + area-specific tuning' reading, not 'identical circuits.' (2) The layer-specific I/O and FF/FB laminar rules give the module's interface spec: L4/L6 as feedforward sensory input ports, L2/3 as the object/feature output and lateral cortico-cortical voting channel (Thousand Brains 'voting' across columns maps naturally onto long-range L2/3 IT connections), L5 as motor/behavioral output and L6 as feedback/state — a natural place to attach the reference-frame (location) signal Hawkins et al. propose (grid-cell-like coding in cortex, Hawkins et al. 2019 Front Neural Circuits). ## Dendritic computation and single-neuron complexity

Over the past ~25 years, experimental and computational neuroscience has established that cortical pyramidal neurons are not passive point summators but multi-compartment nonlinear devices: their dendrites host regenerative events (Na+, Ca2+, and especially NMDA spikes), and functionally distinct integration zones (apical tuft vs. basal/perisomatic) let a single neuron act as a coincidence detector and a small multi-layer network. Two convergent framings dominate — Larkum's biophysical two-site apical/basal coupling model of the L5 pyramidal cell, and Poirazi & Mel's abstraction of the dendritic tree as a two-layer artificial neural network — culminating in Beniaguev, Segev & London (2021), who showed a deep temporally-convolutional network (~5-8 layers) is needed to emulate one L5 pyramidal cell, with NMDA nonlinearity as the driver of that depth. The upshot: the cortical "unit of computation" is closer to a mini deep network than a single sigmoid.

**What is established.**

**1. Dendrites are electrically active and support local spikes.** Three regenerative dendritic events are well characterized: fast Na+ spikes, apical Ca2+ (Ca2+ plateau) spikes, and NMDA-receptor-mediated spikes. Schiller, Major, Koester & Schiller (2000, Nature) gave direct evidence that co-activation of clustered neighboring basal inputs triggers local NMDA spikes (NMDARs contributing ~80% of the charge), amplifying the somatic response by ~2-fold. NMDA spikes are the dominant nonlinear drive in thin (basal/oblique/tuft) dendrites, which receive the majority of cortical synapses (Major, Larkum & Schiller 2013, Annu. Rev. Neurosci.).

**2. The L5 pyramidal cell has two coupled spike-initiation zones (Larkum two-site model).** Larkum, Zhu & Sakmann (1999, Nature) showed the apical tuft has its own Ca2+ spike initiation zone, electrically remote from the axo-somatic Na+ zone. The two are coupled by "BAC firing" (Backpropagation-Activated Ca2+ spike): a backpropagating axonal AP that coincides with a subthreshold distal EPSP crosses the apical Ca2+ threshold, generating a Ca2+ spike that drives a somatic burst. This makes the L5 cell a coincidence detector that associates feedforward input (basal, cortical layers 4/5) with feedback/top-down input (apical tuft in layer 1). Larkum (2013, Trends Neurosci.) elevated this to an "associative organizing principle" for cortex, matched to its laminar architecture and gated by inhibitory microcircuits.

**3. The dendritic tree behaves as a two-layer neural network (Poirazi-Mel).** Poirazi, Brannon & Mel (2003, Neuron, "Pyramidal neuron as two-layer neural network") showed a detailed CA1 compartmental model's firing rate is well predicted by a two-layer abstraction: each long thin terminal dendrite acts as an independently-thresholded sigmoidal subunit (layer 1), whose outputs are summed and thresholded at the soma (layer 2). Subthreshold responses to paired inputs are a sum of nonlinear subunit responses. This "dendritic subunit" view is the conceptual bridge from biophysics to ANN-style abstraction.

**4. Single human/rodent neurons can solve nonlinearly-separable problems.** Gidon et al. (2020, Science) discovered graded calcium-mediated dendritic action potentials (dCaAPs) in human layer 2/3 pyramidal neurons — uniquely, amplitude is maximal at threshold and dampens for stronger input — enabling a single neuron's dendrites to compute XOR (linearly non-separable), a task classically thought to require multilayer networks.

**5. A single cortical neuron requires a deep network to emulate (Beniaguev et al. 2021).** Training DNNs to reproduce the millisecond-resolution input/output of biophysical models: an L5 pyramidal cell with NMDA synapses needs a temporally-convolutional network of ~5-8 layers (they report 7) with ~1000s of artificial units; with NMDARs ablated, a single-hidden-layer network suffices. NMDA-driven synaptic nonlinearity is the specific source of computational "depth." Interpretation: cortical networks are computationally deeper than their anatomical pre-to-post wiring suggests; dendritic integration reads out as spatiotemporal pattern matching against learned templates.

**6. General framing.** London & Häusser (2005, Annu. Rev. Neurosci., "Dendritic Computation") and Stuart & Spruston (2015, Nat. Neurosci., "Dendritic integration: 60 years of progress") consolidated the field: dendrites implement a repertoire of linear and nonlinear elementary computations (low-pass filtering, coincidence detection, direction selectivity, logical operations, gain control), and the contribution of single-neuron intrinsic excitability to brain computation had been historically underestimated.

**Open questions / controversies.**

- **How much of the emulation "depth" is real cortical computation vs. an artifact of the modeling choice?** The 5-8 layer figure is the depth of an ANN needed to fit a *biophysical simulation* at ms resolution; how much of that expressivity the brain actually exploits behaviorally (vs. it being latent capacity) is unsettled. NMDA-driven complexity is robust across studies, but the exact "effective number of independent subunits" per neuron in vivo is debated.
- **Subunit independence and its limits.** Whether dendritic branches integrate truly independently (Poirazi/Mel) or interact strongly depends on morphology, branch coupling, and background activity (e.g. Behabadi/Mel; "Mechanisms underlying subunit independence," PNAS 2014). Apical length/morphology tunes the regime (bioRxiv/eLife "Apical length governs computational diversity").
- **In vivo relevance.** How reliably dendritic spikes (NMDA/Ca2+) occur during natural behavior, and their causal role in perception/learning, is still being pinned down (Stuart & Spruston flag the gap between slice and intact brain; Larkum's apical-amplification-as-consciousness hypothesis is provocative and unproven).
- **Do biological constraints (binary branching, input repetition, fixed excitability) help or hurt computation?** Jones & Kording (2021) and "Do biological constraints impair dendritic computation?" (2022) reach nuanced answers — repetition of inputs across branches helps; strict binary trees limit performance.
- **Generality across cell types and species.** Most quantitative claims come from L5 (thick-tufted) and CA1; how the two-site model and dCaAPs generalize across L2/3, other species, and interneurons is incompletely mapped. dCaAPs were reported in human L2/3 but their prevalence elsewhere is open.
- **Learning rules for dendritic subunits.** How synaptic plasticity assigns inputs to the "right" dendritic subunit to exploit this capacity (structured/clustered plasticity, dendritic credit assignment / backprop approximations) is an active frontier.

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

This sub-field is arguably the strongest biophysical justification for a "grow-the-architecture" thesis in a Thousand Brains-style functional cortical model, for three reasons:

**1. Active dendrites are the substrate of prediction.** The Thousand Brains theory's neuron is Hawkins & Ahmad's (2016, Front. Neural Circuits, "Why neurons have thousands of synapses") active-dendrite model: distal dendritic segments act as coincidence detectors that, when ~8-20 synapses co-activate, generate an NMDA-spike-like depolarization that puts the cell in a *predictive (depolarized-but-not-firing) state*. That is exactly the NMDA-spike biophysics from Schiller 2000 / Major-Larkum-Schiller 2013. So the functional model's core "prediction = distal dendritic recognition of context" is directly grounded in this literature — proximal/basal synapses = feedforward drive, distal/apical = context/feedback, mirroring Larkum's two-site apical/basal split.

**2. The unit of computation should be a mini-network, not a point neuron.** Beniaguev-Segev-London (2021) and Gidon et al. (2020) say a single pyramidal cell already implements a multilayer, XOR-capable computation. For anyone building a cortical column model, this argues the atomic element should be a multi-compartment neuron with many semi-independent dendritic subunits (each a pattern-matcher over a sparse synaptic template) — i.e., you "grow the architecture" by making each cell a small network, dramatically raising per-column representational capacity and the number of contexts/objects a column can hold and predict. This is precisely how HTM/Numenta implement columns (many dendritic segments per cell, each recognizing a distinct SDR context).

**3. It reframes cortical depth.** Beniaguev's conclusion — cortical networks are "deeper than they look" because each anatomical node hides ~7 effective layers — suggests a Thousand Brains model that treats neurons as point units is under-provisioned. A biologically faithful grow-the-architecture design would add dendritic-subunit nonlinearity (clustered synapses + local thresholds) as an explicit layer between synapse and soma, rather than more point neurons. Caveat (low-confidence editorializing): the 7-layer number is emulation-fidelity depth, not a proof the cortex *uses* all of it, so use it as a capacity argument, not a hard architectural spec.

## Predictive coding / predictive processing

Predictive coding proposes that cortex is a hierarchical generative model in which feedback connections convey predictions of lower-level activity and feedforward connections convey the residual prediction error, with the whole system tuned to minimize error (formalized by Friston as minimizing variational free energy / active inference). The computational framework (Rao-Ballard 1999) and its normative Bayesian formulation are mature and highly influential, and there is solid evidence for top-down modulation of sensory cortex, expectation-related response reduction, mismatch signals, and a laminar/oscillatory feedforward-feedback asymmetry. What remains genuinely contested is the strong-form claim that cortex contains dedicated "prediction-error units" wired in a canonical microcircuit — the direct neural evidence is patchy, confounded by adaptation and attention, and several core predictions have not been cleanly confirmed.

**What is established.**

SETTLED / WELL-SUPPORTED:
- The computational model exists and works: Rao & Ballard (1999, Nat Neurosci) showed a hierarchical network with feedback-carries-prediction / feedforward-carries-error can learn simple-cell receptive fields and reproduce extra-classical (end-stopping, surround-suppression) effects as emergent error responses. This is a settled result about the model, not about the brain.
- Normative formalization is mature: Friston (2005, Phil Trans R Soc B "A theory of cortical responses"; 2010, Nat Rev Neurosci "The free-energy principle") recast predictive coding as approximate Bayesian (empirical-Bayes) inference — the Rao-Ballard energy = variational free energy — and extended it to action (active inference). The math and the family of models are settled; whether the brain implements THIS objective is not.
- Anatomical feedforward/feedback asymmetry is real and laminar-specific (Felleman & Van Essen 1991; Markov/Kennedy): feedforward originates superficial → terminates L4; feedback originates deep/superficial → terminates L1 and deep layers. Bastos et al. (2012, Neuron) mapped this onto a "canonical microcircuit for predictive coding," assigning superficial pyramidal cells to error and deep pyramidal cells to prediction — the anatomy is solid; the functional assignment is a hypothesis.
- Oscillatory signature of directionality: gamma (feedforward, superficial layers) vs alpha/beta (feedback, deep layers) replicated in monkey (van Kerkoerle et al. 2014, PNAS; Bastos et al. 2015, Neuron) and human (Michalareas et al. 2016, Neuron). Robust as a directionality marker; the "alpha=prediction, gamma=error" labeling is an interpretation, not proof.
- Cellular substrate for top-down integration is well established: L5 pyramidal neurons have apical tufts in L1 that receive cortico-cortical/higher-order feedback; coincident basal (feedforward) + apical (feedback) input triggers dendritic Ca2+ spikes and burst firing (BAC firing) — Larkum, Zhu & Sakmann (1999, Nature); Larkum (2013, Trends Neurosci "A cellular mechanism for cortical associations"). The compartmentalized apical dendrite is a natural site to compute a prediction/error comparison. That apical dendrites gate feedback is settled; that they literally compute a predictive-coding error term is a live hypothesis (Mikulasch et al. 2023).
- Expectation modulates early sensory cortex: expectation suppression (reduced mean response to predicted stimuli) is robust across fMRI/EEG/MEG (Summerfield, de Lange). Kok, Jehee & de Lange (2012, Neuron) showed expectation simultaneously REDUCES the mean V1 BOLD response yet SHARPENS the decodable stimulus representation — an influential, replicated dissociation.
- Mismatch / prediction-error phenomena exist: (a) Auditory mismatch negativity (MMN) scales with stimulus predictability and is well modeled as a prediction-error signal (Garrido, Kilner, Stephan & Friston 2009, Clin Neurophysiol; Wacongne et al. 2012). (b) Mouse V1 contains neurons that fire to visuomotor mismatch (halting expected optic flow) — Keller, Bonhoeffer & Hübener (2012, Neuron); Zmarz & Keller (2016); Attinger, Wang & Keller (2017, Cell) showed these responses are learned from visuomotor coupling during development and depend on SST interneuron-mediated inhibition. O'Toole, Oyibo & Keller (2023, Neuron) showed positive vs negative prediction-error responses map onto distinct molecularly defined L2/3 cell types (Adamts2, Rrad) — the strongest recent evidence that error responses are a real, targetable feature.

**Open questions / controversies.**

CONTESTED / UNRESOLVED:
- Do dedicated "prediction-error units" exist as a canonical cortical feature? This is the central live controversy. Walsh et al. (2020) and Kogo & Trengove (2015, "Is predictive coding theory articulated enough to be testable?") argue the direct evidence is thin and the strong one-to-one representation-unit/error-unit wiring predicted by Bastos et al. is not clearly observed anatomically. Keller-lab mouse data (2012-2023) are the best counter-evidence, but critics note confounds (see below).
- Adaptation confound: much "expectation suppression" and the MMN can be explained by simpler stimulus-specific adaptation / repetition suppression rather than genuine top-down prediction. Disentangling them (e.g., May & Tiitinen; Todorovic & de Lange 2012) is unsettled; model-comparison (DCM) favoring connectivity changes is suggestive but not decisive.
- Attention vs prediction: expectation and attention have opposing effects on sensory gain (Kok et al. 2012, Cereb Cortex "Attention reverses the effect of prediction"); precision-weighting is invoked to reconcile them, but where/how precision (gain on error) is neurally implemented (NMDA, cholinergic, pulvinar, interneuron gain) is unresolved.
- Sharpening vs dampening: whether expectation reduces, sharpens, or biases representations depends on paradigm; a single mechanistic account that predicts which occurs is missing.
- Mouse "mismatch" interpretation: are these signals true hierarchical prediction errors, or motor/arousal/locomotion signals, off-responses, or negative-image responses? The playback-vs-coupling controls are debated.
- Falsifiability of the free-energy principle: widely criticized as too general to be falsified — it constrains the form of models but arguably makes few unique, risky predictions (Walsh et al. 2020; Colombo & Wright; Gershman critiques).
- Feedback sign: predictive coding requires feedback to (partly) SUPPRESS lower-level activity, but much feedback is empirically facilitatory/amplifying (figure-ground enhancement, attentional gain). How suppression and facilitation coexist is unresolved.
- Where is the error computed? Somatic separate populations (classic PC) vs local dendritic compartments (Mikulasch et al. 2023, Trends Neurosci; Sacramento et al. 2018) vs subthreshold membrane potential — an active reframing that would rescue PC from the "missing error units" problem but is not yet directly confirmed.
- Learning rule: whether cortex approximates backprop via predictive coding (Whittington & Bogacz 2017; Millidge et al.) or uses a distinct local rule is open.
- Recent work (e.g., 2023-2026 large-scale and cell-type-resolved recordings) is actively testing these; consensus has NOT been reached as of 2026.

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

Bearing on the Thousand Brains Project (TBP / Numenta, Hawkins) and a grow-the-architecture thesis:
- Shared premise, different mechanism. Both TBP and predictive coding assert (i) cortex is fundamentally PREDICTIVE — it continuously predicts its own input — and (ii) a single "canonical" computation repeats across cortex. This is the strongest point of contact: predictive coding supplies TBP with a normative objective (minimize prediction error / free energy) and a laminar-circuit + oscillatory + cellular substrate that TBP's column model largely leaves unspecified.
- Dendrites as the unifying substrate. HTM/TBP already treats distal/basal dendrites as prediction units: coincident lateral+contextual input puts a neuron into a depolarized "predictive state," and unpredicted input causes mini-column bursting (a mismatch signal). This converges strikingly with (a) Larkum's apical-dendrite/BAC-firing feedback integration and (b) Mikulasch et al.'s (2023) "prediction error computed locally in dendritic compartments." A grow-the-architecture thesis can fuse them: implement hierarchical prediction-error at the dendritic-compartment level rather than as separate somatic error populations — which simultaneously answers PC's biggest empirical weakness (missing dedicated error units) and gives TBP a quantitative error/precision signal.
- Complementary gaps. Predictive coding is strong on hierarchy, Bayesian objective, and error/precision but weak on reference frames, object identity, and sensorimotor "where" signals. TBP is strong on reference frames, location coding, lateral voting across columns, and object-centric models but weak on a normative learning objective and laminar mechanism. A merged architecture (hierarchical prediction-error + grid/reference-frame location code + lateral voting + precision-weighted attention) is a natural grow-the-architecture target.
- The contested status of "error units" is GOOD news for TBP. Because TBP does not posit dedicated feedforward error populations — it posits columns entering predictive states and mismatch-driven bursting — the empirical difficulty of finding canonical PC error units, and the dendritic-error reframing, both tilt toward TBP-compatible implementations. Precision-weighting in PC maps onto attentional/voting-based gain allocation in TBP.
- Caution: predictive coding's strict feedback=suppression assumption conflicts with facilitatory feedback; TBP's lateral-voting feedback is amplificatory. A unified model must handle both signs of top-down influence — an open design constraint worth flagging.

## Grid cells, place cells and cognitive maps

The hippocampal-entorhinal system encodes a metric "cognitive map" of space through place cells (hippocampus, O'Keefe & Dostrovsky 1971) and grid cells (medial entorhinal cortex, Hafting/Moser 2005, Nobel Prize 2014). Over the last decade this map has been shown to be domain-general: the same circuit and the same grid-like hexadirectional code map non-spatial continuous dimensions (sound, abstract 2D concept spaces, social hierarchies), and computational models (Tolman-Eichenbaum Machine, successor representation) recast grid/place cells as reusable structural bases that generalize relational knowledge. This "space as a substrate for thought" program is the direct empirical and conceptual foundation for grid-cells-in-neocortex theories like the Thousand Brains framework.

**What is established.**

SPATIAL CORE (well-settled):
- Place cells: hippocampal (CA1/CA3) pyramidal cells fire in a circumscribed "place field"; the population tiles an environment and "remaps" (rate/global) between contexts. First reported by O'Keefe & Dostrovsky (1971); theorized as a "cognitive map" in O'Keefe & Nadel's 1978 book. Theta phase precession (O'Keefe & Recce 1993) links firing to a temporal/sequence code.
- Grid cells: layer II medial entorhinal cortex (mEC) neurons fire on a periodic hexagonal (triangular) lattice tiling all explored space, invariant to sensory detail. Discovered by the Moser lab (Fyhn 2004 precursor; Hafting et al. 2005). Organized into a small number of discrete MODULES of increasing spatial scale along the dorsoventral mEC axis (Stensola et al. 2012, Nature). Population activity lives on a torus (Gardner et al. 2022, Nature) — strong support for continuous-attractor-network (CAN) models over the older oscillatory-interference account.
- A cell-type "zoo" supports the map: head-direction cells, border/boundary-vector cells (Solstad et al. 2008; Lever/Burgess BVCs), conjunctive grid x head-direction cells (Sargolini et al. 2006), object-vector cells (Høydal et al. 2019), speed cells (Kropff et al. 2015), and hippocampal "time cells" (Eichenbaum, MacDonald). Grid cells are widely read as a path-integration / metric substrate; place cells as conjunctive, context-bound bindings.
- Human evidence: a hexadirectional (six-fold symmetric) fMRI adaptation signal in entorhinal cortex during virtual navigation (Doeller, Barry & Burgess 2010, Nature); direct human grid-like single-unit recordings (Jacobs et al. 2013, Nat Neurosci).

EXTENSION BEYOND SPACE (active, increasingly established):
- The hippocampal-entorhinal circuit maps NON-SPATIAL continuous dimensions with the SAME cell types: sound frequency along a 1D "acoustic" axis (Aronov, Nevers & Tank 2017, Nature); an abstract 2D "bird space" (morphing neck/leg length) elicits the grid-like hexadirectional fMRI code in EC AND ventromedial/medial prefrontal cortex (Constantinescu, O'Reilly & Behrens 2016, Science); social hierarchies as 2D maps (Tavares et al. 2015, Neuron; Park et al. 2021, Nat Neurosci, using a grid-like code for inference); odor, value, and other task dimensions.
- Unifying theory: the "cognitive map" is recast as relational/structural knowledge (Behrens et al. 2018, Neuron). Grid cells = a factorized structural basis that GENERALIZES; place cells = conjunctions of that structure with sensory content. The Tolman-Eichenbaum Machine (Whittington et al. 2020, Cell) instantiates this as a single generative/learning model that spontaneously produces grid-, band-, border-, and object-vector-like entorhinal codes and place-like remapping hippocampal codes, and transfers structure across environments; TEM was later shown to be mathematically close to Transformer self-attention (Whittington et al. 2021/2022).
- Predictive-map account: place cells encode a SUCCESSOR REPRESENTATION (expected future state occupancy) and grid cells approximate its low-dimensional eigenvectors, explaining reward/goal sensitivity and geometry-dependent field distortions (Stachenfeld, Botvinick & Gershman 2017, Nat Neurosci).

- Grid MECHANISM: CAN vs oscillatory-interference is largely settled toward CAN (toroidal manifold, Gardner et al. 2022), but the developmental/learning ORIGIN of grids and how modules acquire their discrete scales remain open (Dong & Fiete 2024 review).
- INTERPRETING human/abstract "grid-like" fMRI: is the hexadirectional signal actually generated by grid cells, or is it an emergent population statistic? Analysis choices and alternative accounts (e.g., clustering/anisotropy) are debated (Nau, Doeller and others).
- What do PLACE cells really compute — geometry, a predictive successor map, reward/goal, or conjunctive episodic memory? Splitter cells, goal-vector cells, and reward remapping complicate a pure-space reading.
- GENERALIZATION/transfer: TEM and the SR are models, but the biological learning rule that factorizes structure from content and reuses it across domains is unknown.
- DIMENSIONALITY & geometry: grids are elegant in flat 2D; evidence in 3D is weaker/less regular (bats — Ginosar et al. 2021), and how (or whether) a hexagonal metric extends to >2D, non-metric, hierarchical/graph/tree, or discrete relational spaces is unresolved. Many abstract "spaces" may be graphs, not continuous manifolds.
- NEOCORTICAL grid cells: whether grid-like reference frames exist throughout neocortex (the strong Thousand Brains claim) is largely hypothesis — direct neocortical grid-cell recordings are scarce; most non-EC evidence is human fMRI in mPFC/vmPFC.
- Links between the map, hippocampal REPLAY, planning, and imagination/simulation are actively being worked out.

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

This literature is the direct scientific bedrock of the Thousand Brains framework and any "grow-the-architecture" thesis built on cortical reference frames. Numenta's proposal (Hawkins, Lewis, Klukas, Purdy & Ahmad 2019, "A Framework for Intelligence and Cortical Function Based on Grid Cells in the Neocortex," Frontiers in Neural Circuits; and Lewis et al. 2019, "Locations in the Neocortex," Frontiers) explicitly generalizes entorhinal grid/place coding: every cortical column is hypothesized to contain grid-cell-like machinery implementing object-centered reference frames, so that the neocortex models objects (and concepts) the way the hippocampal-entorhinal system models space, with predicted "displacement cells" enabling compositional objects. The empirical results here are what make that plausible: (1) grid/place codes map NON-spatial and ABSTRACT spaces (Aronov 2017; Constantinescu 2016; Bellmund 2018), so reusing the code as a general modeling primitive is not a stretch; (2) TEM (Whittington 2020) and the successor representation (Stachenfeld 2017) give concrete, TRAINABLE instantiations of the two transferable building blocks — a factorized structural/relational basis (grid-like) plus conjunctive content binding (place-like) that generalizes across tasks — exactly the kind of module a growable architecture could add as a "structural memory / reference-frame" layer, and TEM's proven equivalence to Transformer attention hints the primitive already lives inside modern sequence models. Caveats to carry: neocortical grid cells remain largely a hypothesis (weak direct evidence outside EC/mPFC); the leap from a 2D hexagonal metric to arbitrary graph/hierarchical cognitive spaces is unresolved; and human grid-like signals are indirect fMRI proxies. So the TBP direction is well-motivated by this field but not yet empirically closed at the cortical level.

## The Thousand Brains Theory and reference frames

The Thousand Brains Theory (TBT), developed by Jeff Hawkins and colleagues at Numenta, proposes that the neocortex implements a single repeated algorithm in which every cortical column learns complete, location-based (reference-frame) models of whole objects using grid-cell-like coordinate systems, rather than columns extracting progressively complex features up a hierarchy. Perception arises when thousands of such columnar models — across and within sensory modalities — reach consensus by voting over long-range cortico-cortical connections. The theory is an explicit modern instantiation of Mountcastle's "common cortical algorithm" idea, extending entorhinal/hippocampal grid-cell machinery (Moser & Moser) to all of cortex; it is influential and generative but empirically unproven on its most specific claim (single-unit grid cells in sensory neocortex) and sits inside a live debate about cortical uniformity.

**What is established.**

CORE CLAIMS OF THE FRAMEWORK (Hawkins et al. 2019, Frontiers in Neural Circuits):
- Grid-cell-like neurons exist in EVERY cortical column, not only in entorhinal cortex. Whereas entorhinal grid cells represent the animal's body location in an environment, cortical grid cells are proposed to represent the location of sensed features relative to an object (an object-centric reference frame), and to represent many objects/locations simultaneously.
- Every cortical column learns models of COMPLETE objects (not just features). A column combines sensory input (e.g. from L4) with a grid-cell-derived location signal and integrates over sensor movement via path integration (using motor efference copies), the same mechanism the entorhinal cortex uses for navigation. This makes cortical computation fundamentally sensorimotor.
- Because many columns each build models of the same object, there are thousands of redundant models distributed across cortex ("thousand brains"). They resolve ambiguity and reach a single percept by VOTING: long-range, non-hierarchical lateral/cortico-cortical connections let columns communicate a stable object identity while their location/feature representations still differ, giving fast consensus across touch, vision, etc. This reframes hierarchy as sensor fusion among complete models rather than feature convergence at a single "top."
- Specific anatomical predictions: cortical grid cells associated with L6 (reciprocal with L4); NEW "displacement cells" in L5 encoding relative positions between objects/components (displacement vectors) to support compositional/hierarchical object structure; an orientation ("head-direction"-like) signal. The same reference-frame machinery is proposed to underlie abstract thought and concepts, not just physical objects.

LINEAGE OF THE ARGUMENT (three Numenta papers build the case in order):
- 2017 (Hawkins, Ahmad, Cui): a multi-column network in which each column integrates changing input over time to learn predictive object models; lateral connections across columns speed inference. It did NOT yet specify how object-centric location is computed.
- 2019a (Lewis, Purdy, Ahmad, Hawkins, "Locations in the Neocortex"): supplies the missing neural mechanism — a two-layer network using cortical grid cells + path integration to represent sensor location on an object and robustly learn/recognize objects through movement.
- 2019b (Hawkins, Lewis, Klukas, Purdy, Ahmad): the umbrella "Framework"/Thousand Brains paper that generalizes reference frames + grid cells + voting to all cortex and to cognition.

EMPIRICAL BACKDROP THE THEORY LEANS ON (independent of Numenta):
- Grid cells discovered in rodent entorhinal cortex (Hafting, Fyhn, Molden, M-B Moser, E. Moser, 2005, Nature) — hexagonally periodic firing; 2014 Nobel Prize (O'Keefe; M-B & E. Moser).
- Human grid-like activity recorded directly (Jacobs et al. 2013, Nat Neurosci) — most grid-like cells in entorhinal AND cingulate cortex, hinting grid coding is not confined to entorhinal cortex.
- Grid-like codes for NON-spatial, conceptual knowledge in humans (Constantinescu, O'Reilly, Behrens 2016, Science) — a hexagonal fMRI signal when navigating an abstract 2D concept space, supporting the idea that reference-frame/grid machinery generalizes to cognition. (Background, not verified this session: Doeller, Barry & Burgess 2010, Nature, gave earlier human fMRI evidence for entorhinal grid-like signals — treat exact details as lower confidence.)

ENGINEERING INSTANTIATION:
- The Thousand Brains Project (spun out of Numenta into an independent non-profit, partly Gates Foundation-funded, 2024) released Monty, an open-source sensorimotor learning framework (named after Mountcastle). Monty is built from repeated "learning modules" (the cortical-column analog) that do 3D object recognition + pose estimation with continual, sample-efficient learning and voting-based consensus. Reported strengths are rapid/continual learning and robustness rather than benchmark-topping accuracy vs deep nets.

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

This IS the functional cortical model at the heart of a 'grow-the-architecture' thesis. TBT's key move is that intelligence is not a monolithic network but a large population of near-identical repeated units (cortical columns / 'learning modules') each running the same reference-frame + grid-cell + sensorimotor-prediction algorithm, composed by lateral voting rather than a deep feature hierarchy. That makes the architecture inherently GROWABLE by module replication: capacity and robustness scale by adding more columnar learning modules and voting connections, and modalities/objects/concepts are added by instantiating new reference frames — not by retraining a global function. Monty (arXiv:2412.18354, 2507.04494) is the concrete substrate for this thesis: a repeated learning-module you can scale out, with continual/online learning and consensus built in. The Mountcastle common-algorithm premise is exactly what licenses 'one module, repeated' as the growth primitive (and the same premise is the main point of contention, per Marcus et al. 2014). Relevant nuance for a grow-the-architecture argument: voting/consensus and displacement/compositional structure are the mechanisms by which many small models compose into a larger capability, and the biggest open risk to the thesis is empirical (do sensory columns really carry grid/reference-frame codes?) plus the scaling question (does module repetition reach general competence?).

## Neocortex development and arealization

Neocortical arealization is the process by which an initially near-uniform neuroepithelium is subdivided into functionally distinct areas (V1, S1, M1, etc.), each built from the same canonical six-layered microcircuit. The field's organizing debate — Rakic's intrinsic protomap (areal identity prefigured in progenitors) vs O'Leary's extrinsic protocortex (identity imposed later by thalamic input) — is now settled as a synthesis: patterning-center morphogens set up graded transcription-factor expression that gives progenitors coarse positional identity, and thalamocortical activity refines and sharpens the resulting boundaries. Increasingly the whole process is framed as "algorithmic growth" — a compact genomic program plus gradients plus activity that constructs, rather than blueprints, the cortical sheet.

**What is established.**

- **Radial Unit Hypothesis (Rakic).** Excitatory neurons are generated in the ventricular zone (VZ) and migrate along radial glial fibers into the cortical plate, stacking in an inside-out sequence (early-born deep layers first, late-born superficial layers last). The radial glial fiber defines an "ontogenetic column" — Rakic proposed the column as the fundamental ontogenetic and evolutionary unit of the cortex, and that tangential cortical area/size scales with the NUMBER of radial units while thickness scales with cells per unit.
- **Radial glia are the neural stem cells.** Live imaging (Noctor & Kriegstein 2001, 2004) overturned the older view of radial glia as mere guide scaffold: radial glia divide asymmetrically to self-renew and generate neurons, either directly or via transit-amplifying intermediate progenitor cells (IPCs, Tbr2/Eomes+) in the subventricular zone. This is the cellular engine of the protomap.
- **Protomap hypothesis (Rakic 1988).** The VZ carries an intrinsic "proto-map" of prospective cytoarchitectonic areas, established before thalamic afferents arrive — areal identity is at least partly specified in progenitors.
- **Protocortex hypothesis (van der Loos & Woolsey; O'Leary 1989).** The nascent cortex is initially equipotent/uniform ("tabula rasa"); area identity is imposed extrinsically by thalamocortical afferents (TCAs). Supported by classic rewiring/transplant experiments (e.g., Sur's rewiring of retinal input to auditory thalamus inducing visual-like maps; O'Leary's heterotopic transplants).
- **Patterning centers + morphogen gradients.** Signaling centers at the rim of the dorsal telencephalon secrete morphogens that pattern the field: the anterior commissural/rostral patterning center (FGF8, FGF17), the cortical hem (Wnts, BMPs), and an antihem laterally. FGF8 acts as a graded diffusible signal (Fukuchi-Shimogori & Grove 2001): raising anterior FGF8 shifts area boundaries posteriorly, lowering it shifts them anteriorly, and an ectopic posterior FGF8 source duplicates area identity (ectopic barrel field).
- **Graded transcription factors convert position into identity.** Four TFs with opposing graded expression across cortical axes specify area identity in progenitors: Pax6 (high rostral-lateral), Emx2 (high caudal-medial), COUP-TFI/Nr2f1 (high caudal), and Sp8 (high rostral). Loss/gain-of-function shifts and rescales areas (Bishop, Goudreau & O'Leary 2000; O'Leary, Chou & Sahara 2007). FGF8 patterns in part by repressing Emx2. The protomap is propagated from VZ progenitors to cortical-plate neurons through an Eomes/Tbr2-dependent intermediate stage.
- **Modern synthesis.** Intrinsic gradients establish coarse "proto-areas" among excitatory neurons; TCAs and neural activity then refine, sharpen, and maintain functional area boundaries. Intrinsic and extrinsic mechanisms are complementary, not exclusive.
- **Interneuron origin is separate.** GABAergic interneurons are born in the ventral telencephalon (medial/caudal ganglionic eminences) and reach the cortex by long-distance TANGENTIAL migration, then switch to radial migration to their layer — a fundamentally different developmental logic from the radially-generated excitatory neurons (Marín & Rubenstein).
- **Human/primate expansion via outer radial glia.** An enlarged outer subventricular zone (OSVZ) populated by outer radial glia (oRG/bRG), which self-amplify via mitotic somal translocation and feed IPCs, underlies the disproportionate expansion and gyrification of human/gyrencephalic cortex (Hansen & Kriegstein 2010; Fietz & Huttner).
- **Single-cell transcriptomics is reframing arealization.** Recent atlases (Nowakowski, Bhaduri, Kriegstein labs) show areal molecular signatures in fetal cortex are relatively modest/gradient-like and emerge dynamically over developmental time rather than as sharp early compartments — consistent with graded serial-homology models over a discrete early mosaic.

**Open questions / controversies.**

- **Where exactly is the intrinsic/extrinsic balance in humans?** Rodent gradient experiments are clean, but the relative weight of intrinsic protomap vs thalamic/activity refinement in the far larger, later-maturing human cortex is unresolved.
- **Map vs gradient.** Single-cell data suggest fetal areal identity is graded and emergent rather than a sharply compartmentalized early mosaic. Is the 'protomap' a true map, or a set of overlapping gradients that only later resolve into discrete areas (serial homology)? How graded TF fields sharpen into crisp cytoarchitectonic borders is still mechanistically open.
- **Boundary sharpening.** The molecular logic converting continuous morphogen/TF gradients into discrete, reproducible area boundaries (roles of cross-repression, Notch, activity-dependent gene programs) is incompletely specified.
- **oRG and area-specific expansion.** How outer radial glia amplification is regionally tuned to produce area-specific differences in size/neuron number — and whether this is intrinsic or feedback-controlled — remains unclear.
- **Interneuron–excitatory matching.** How tangentially-arriving interneurons acquire area- and layer-appropriate identity: instructed by the local excitatory protomap, intrinsically pre-specified in the ganglionic eminences, or both?
- **Causal role of activity/thalamus.** The precise developmental window and mechanism by which thalamocortical activity instructs vs merely refines area identity is debated; subplate's role as intermediary is still being mapped.
- **Evolution and scaling.** What genomic changes expand radial-unit number (surface area) largely independent of thickness, and how the compact genome encodes area-specific wiring — the crux Zador's genomic-bottleneck argument raises but does not resolve.

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

This sub-field is close to a biological existence proof for a grow-the-architecture thesis, and maps cleanly onto the Thousand Brains framework's core commitment that the cortex is a tiled array of near-identical, repeated computational units (cortical columns) running one general algorithm.

1. **The column as the repeated unit.** Rakic's radial unit hypothesis makes the ontogenetic column the fundamental developmental and evolutionary building block, and says cortical AREA scales with the NUMBER of such units. This is the developmental counterpart of TBP/Mountcastle uniformity: you enlarge and diversify cortex by replicating a canonical column, not by designing each area from scratch. A grow-the-architecture model should likewise scale by instantiating more copies of a shared column module.

2. **Arealization = same circuit, gradient-tuned.** The synthesis view — a common microcircuit whose identity is set by a handful of morphogen/TF gradients (FGF8, Wnt/BMP; Pax6/Emx2/COUP-TFI/Sp8) and then refined by input — is a concrete recipe for 'one algorithm, many specializations.' For TBP this suggests area differences are lightweight parametric/positional modulations of a shared column, not distinct architectures, and that a small set of global gradients (a coordinate/positional-encoding signal) can differentiate an otherwise uniform sheet.

3. **Protomap vs protocortex ≈ innate priors vs learned tuning.** The intrinsic protomap is an innate structural prior (wiring the model starts with); protocortex/thalamic refinement is experience/activity-driven tuning. The resolved synthesis — coarse innate scaffold + activity-dependent refinement — is a direct template for how a grown TBP-style architecture should combine genomically-cheap innate structure with reference-frame learning.

4. **Algorithmic growth / genomic bottleneck is the deepest tie-in.** Zador's genomic bottleneck and Hiesinger's algorithmic-growth thesis argue the genome specifies a compact GENERATIVE PROGRAM (rules, gradients, local interactions), not a connection-by-connection blueprint. That is exactly a grow-the-architecture stance: a small developmental program unfolds into a large, structured, learning-ready network. For a TBP implementation this motivates encoding the architecture as a compact growth/tiling rule (spawn columns, wire by local gradient rules, refine by activity) rather than hand-specifying every column and connection.

Caveat: these mappings are interpretive analogies between developmental neurobiology and the TBP engineering thesis, not claims the biology was designed to validate TBP — treat the bridge as a source of design principles, medium confidence.

## Cortical connectomics at synaptic resolution

Nanoscale serial-section electron microscopy plus automated deep-learning reconstruction has, since 2024-2025, produced the first cubic-millimeter-scale dense connectomes of mammalian cortex: the Harvard/Google H01 fragment of adult human temporal cortex (Shapson-Coe et al., Science 2024) and the multi-lab MICrONS reconstruction of mouse visual cortex co-registered with in vivo calcium imaging (MICrONS Consortium et al., Nature 2025). These datasets confirmed cell-type-specific and function-specific wiring motifs (notably "like-to-like" excitatory connectivity and highly specific inhibitory targeting) while also exposing surprises (rare very-strong multisynaptic connections, a new deep-layer neuron class). They simultaneously sharpened the long-standing argument, associated with Lichtman himself, that a static wiring diagram is necessary but not sufficient to explain function: synaptic sign, weight, dynamics, neuromodulation and plasticity are largely invisible to fixed-tissue EM.

**What is established.**

PETASCALE RECONSTRUCTION IS NOW FEASIBLE. Both projects demonstrate that ~1 mm^3 of cortex (~10^15 voxels, petabyte-to-1.4-petabyte imaging volumes) can be imaged by serial-section EM and densely reconstructed by automated flood-filling/convolutional segmentation, with limited human proofreading, into browsable public resources (h01-release, microns-explorer.org).

H01 (HUMAN, Shapson-Coe et al. 2024): ~1 mm^3 of surgically resected adult human temporal cortex (removed to access an epileptic/hippocampal focus), spanning all six cortical layers. ~1.4 PB of EM data; ~57,000 cells; ~150 million synapses (the online resource annotates ~183 million); reconstruction of thousands of neurons plus glia, vasculature and myelin. Reported structural findings: glia outnumber neurons roughly 2:1 (oligodendrocytes the most common cell type) [medium confidence on exact ratio]; deep-layer excitatory neurons classifiable by dendritic orientation, including a previously unrecognized class of "triangular" directionally-oriented neurons in deep layers; and, among the thousands of weak (typically 1-2 synapse) inputs each neuron receives, rare, extraordinarily powerful axonal inputs making up to ~50 synapses onto a single target (multisynaptic pairs), whose function is unknown. Corresponding authors Jeff W. Lichtman (Harvard) and Viren Jain (Google).

MICrONS (MOUSE, 2025 Nature package): a cubic-millimeter volume of visual cortex (~1.3 x 0.87 x 0.82 mm^3) spanning primary visual cortex (VISp) and higher areas (VISrl/VISal/VISlm), with >200,000 cells and ~523-524 million synapses in the EM reconstruction, co-registered to two-photon calcium imaging of ~75,000 functionally characterized neurons in the awake mouse viewing natural/synthetic stimuli. This is the first millimeter-scale dataset directly linking single-neuron physiology to synaptic-resolution wiring.

WIRING MOTIFS REVEALED: (1) "Like-to-like" excitatory connectivity - neurons with similar visual tuning preferentially synapse onto each other; Ding et al. 2025 showed this generalizes as a "general wiring rule" across layers, across cortical areas, and even along feedback pathways, and that the same rule emerges in task-trained artificial networks where it aids performance. (2) Highly cell-type-specific inhibition (Schneider-Mizell et al. 2025): a connectomic census of ~1,352 cells and >70,000 inhibitory synapses showed inhibitory neurons target specific dendritic/somatic compartments and specific excitatory subclasses - e.g. PV/basket cells target the perisomatic region, Martinotti/SST cells target distal dendrites, VIP cells preferentially disinhibit by targeting other inhibitory cells, and a disinhibitory specialist class targets basket cells. Companion work reported a like-to-like SST->pyramidal motif (similarly tuned SST cells recruited by and suppressing similarly tuned pyramidal cells). Together these establish that cortical microcircuitry follows structured, type- and function-specific rules rather than random or purely distance-based wiring.

**Open questions / controversies.**

(1) Sign and weight from structure: EM gives synapse counts and sizes but not directly excitatory/inhibitory sign, release probability, PSP amplitude or time course; how far synapse size/count proxies functional weight is unsettled (Borst & Leibold; the C. elegans precedent where a 'complete' wiring diagram could not specify signs). (2) What the rare very-strong multisynaptic connections (up to ~50 synapses, H01) actually compute - reinforcement, reliability, priority signals? Unknown. (3) Neuromodulation, plasticity and internal state are invisible to fixed-tissue snapshots - the connectome is a single frozen moment. (4) Long-range inputs from non-imaged regions are truncated, so even a 'dense' mm^3 is an incomplete circuit. (5) n=1 / individual variability: H01 is one epileptic human sample and MICrONS one mouse - which motifs are universal vs idiosyncratic or pathology-related? (6) Proofreading incompleteness and reconstruction errors still bound confident statements about full connectivity. (7) Is like-to-like connectivity causal for tuning, or a byproduct of shared inputs/development? (8) Human-vs-mouse scaling and what, if anything, is human-specific. (9) The deeper conceptual dispute: whether 'understanding' the brain from a connectome is even the right frame (Lichtman: the meaning of 'understanding' must 'undergo an evolution'; the 'promisomics' critique that structure short-circuits mechanistic explanation).

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

These datasets are directly load-bearing for a Thousand Brains-style functional cortical model in two opposite ways. AS SUPPORT: MICrONS confirms that cortex is built from stereotyped, repeatable microcircuit motifs (like-to-like excitation generalizing across layers/areas, and precise cell-type-specific inhibition), which is consistent with TBP's central claim of a common repeated cortical algorithm ('cortical column' as a repeated computational unit) instantiated everywhere with the same wiring template - a 'grow-the-architecture' thesis can point to these as the physical substrate of the repeated column. The laminar targeting rules (L6 specializations in H01, compartment-specific inhibition) also matter for TBP's use of cortical layers to separate sensory input, location/reference-frame signals, and output. AS A CAUTION: the strongest lesson of this sub-field - crystallized in Lichtman/Borst-Leibold - is that a static connectome underdetermines function. TBP is an algorithmic/functional theory (reference frames, sensorimotor prediction, voting), and none of reference frames, grid-cell-like codes, or the learning dynamics it posits are readable off the wiring diagram. So connectomics can validate that the repeated-column substrate and its motifs exist and constrain what the algorithm could be, but it cannot by itself confirm the TBP computation; the theory must be tested against physiology and behavior, with the connectome as a constraint, not a proof. The rare ultra-strong multisynaptic connections and the molecular/neuromodulatory layer are exactly the kind of functionally decisive detail a purely structural, grow-the-architecture reading would miss.

## Large-scale cortical simulation and emulation

This sub-field pursues bottom-up, data-driven digital reconstruction and simulation of cortical tissue, ranging from single microcircuits (~31k neurons) to multi-area and whole-brain aspirations. After two decades and roughly a billion euros/francs across the EPFL Blue Brain Project (2005-2024) and the EU Human Brain Project (2013-2023), the consensus is that detailed reconstructions are impressive engineering and data-integration feats but have NOT produced emergent cognitive function, a validated whole-brain simulation, or a paradigm shift in understanding. The field has quietly split into biophysically-detailed reconstruction (Blue Brain, Allen Institute) versus simplified point-neuron network modeling (Jülich/NEST, Allen GLIF), with an increasingly honest literature admitting whole-cortex emulation remains conceptually — not just computationally — out of reach.

**What is established.**

- **Blue Brain flagship result (2015).** Markram et al. digitally reconstructed and simulated a rat somatosensory cortex microcircuit: ~31,000 neurons in 55 morphological / 207 morpho-electrical types, ~8M connections, ~37-40M synapses, reproducing in-vivo-like emergent activity states and transitions purely from anatomically/physiologically constrained "first principles" (no fitting to the target network activity). This is the field's canonical demonstration that a data-driven reconstruction can yield plausible spontaneous and evoked dynamics.
- **Blue Brain wound down end of 2024** ("mission accomplished" framing), having produced ~300 peer-reviewed papers, >1 petabyte of data, thousands of reconstructed morphologies, and the Blue Brain Open Platform. Its 2024 eLife capstone (Reimann et al.) scaled the approach to the entire non-barrel primary somatosensory cortex (nbS1, 8 subregions): 4.2M morphological neurons, 9.1B local + 4.1B mid-range synapses. Successor: the **Open Brain Institute (OBI)**, a nonprofit launched by Henry & Kamila Markram (March 2025), open-sourcing the simulation "recipe."
- **Human Brain Project delivered infrastructure, not understanding.** HBP (2013-2023, ~€607M of a promised ~€1B) explicitly abandoned its founding "simulate the human brain in 10 years" promise after a 2014-2015 governance revolt and restructuring. It pivoted from atomistic reverse-engineering toward mesoscale/mean-field models, digital twins, and the **EBRAINS** research infrastructure (~3,000 publications, 160+ tools). Official self-assessment claimed 60-70% of specific application promises met; independent critics (Frégnac) judge the paradigmatic "rupture" undelivered.
- **Allen Institute mouse V1 model (Billeh et al. 2020)** is the most rigorously data-integrated cortical model: ~230,000 neurons, 17 cell classes, built in TWO matched variants on the identical connectivity graph — biophysically-detailed compartmental AND GLIF point-neuron — validated against Neuropixels in-vivo recordings (firing rates, orientation/direction selectivity). Key methodological contribution: the point-neuron version reproduces much of the biophysical version's behavior, sharpening the "how much detail is necessary?" question. Tooling: BMTK, SONATA format, GLIF models, all open-source.
- **Point-neuron whole-cortex modeling is a parallel, more tractable tradition.** Potjans & Diesmann (2014) showed layer/cell-type-specific firing rates in a full-density 1 mm² cortical microcircuit are largely explained by connection-count specificity, NOT single-neuron or synapse detail. Schmidt, van Albada et al. (2018) built the first full-density multi-area spiking model (macaque visual cortex, 32 areas) reproducing resting-state spiking statistics and fMRI-like functional connectivity. These run on NEST and neuromorphic hardware (SpiNNaker).
- **Whole-brain simulation is not achieved and timelines are long.** As of 2024 no simulation reaches single-neuron/single-spike resolution over >95% of any mammalian brain during behavior. Igarashi (2025) projects cellular-level mouse whole-brain simulation ~2034, marmoset ~2044, human beyond 2044, bottlenecked by both compute AND measurement data. A Dec-2024 Chinese 86-billion-neuron run on ~14,000 GPUs is widely regarded as too crude to count as emulation.
- **Foundational critique holds.** Jonas & Kording (2017) showed standard neuroscience analysis methods applied to a fully-known MOS 6502 processor failed to yield hierarchical understanding — a direct warning that unlimited data + bottom-up reconstruction does not automatically confer understanding.

**Open questions / controversies.**

- **The abstraction question (central controversy):** how much biophysical detail is actually necessary? Point-neuron models (Potjans-Diesmann; Allen GLIF) reproduce much of what detailed models do, undercutting the maximalist bottom-up premise — yet detailed modelers argue you cannot know what to abstract until you have the detail.
- **Validation / degeneracy:** models have many hand-tuned, under-constrained parameters; multiple parameter sets ("degeneracy") produce similar activity. What counts as a model being "right," and does matching summary statistics constitute validation? Frégnac's warning: virtual data risk becoming self-referential ("studying the properties of the digital Ersatz").
- **The recording gap:** connectome reconstruction is outpacing the ability to record activity across those same neurons, so functional constraints lag structural ones.
- **Does bottom-up reconstruction yield understanding at all?** (Jonas-Kording) — or is a theory/computational-level account required first?
- **Emergent function:** no reconstruction has produced cognition, learning, or behavior beyond low-level dynamics/statistics; whether it can, even in principle, is unsettled.
- **Topology claims** (Reimann 2017 simplices/cavities) remain debated as to whether they are functionally meaningful or artifacts of connectivity statistics.
- **Timeline realism:** Igarashi's 2034/2044+ projections vs. persistent expert skepticism that compute scaling alone closes the gap.

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

This literature is the strongest available cautionary evidence for the Thousand Brains thesis that cortical understanding requires a functional/algorithmic theory, not ever-more biological fidelity. In Marr's terms, Blue Brain and the Allen biophysical models live almost entirely at the implementation level; the honest verdicts of the field — Frégnac ("the paradigmatic rupture remains to be demonstrated"; whole-brain modeling limited by conceptual not just technical gaps) and Jonas-Kording (unlimited data on a known system still fails to yield understanding) — are essentially empirical arguments that a Thousand-Brains-style computational theory is the missing ingredient these megaprojects lacked. Two findings are directly congenial to a repeating-functional-unit, grow-the-architecture thesis: (1) Potjans-Diesmann's result that layer/cell-type activity is explained by connection-count structure rather than molecular detail supports the view that circuit-level organization (columns + connectivity motifs) is the load-bearing abstraction; (2) the Allen matched biophysical-vs-GLIF result shows point-neuron abstractions preserve much functional behavior, licensing TBP's move to model cortical columns at a functional rather than biophysical grain. The megaprojects' failure to get emergent function from atomistic reconstruction is exactly the gap TBP fills by positing a repeating computational unit (the cortical column with reference frames) and composition rules — a "grow the architecture from a validated functional module" strategy rather than "reconstruct every neuron." Finally, the Blue Brain/HBP "simulate the brain in 10 years" overpromise-and-retreat is a governance/communication cautionary tale for any grand cortical-architecture program about calibrating claims to what the theory can actually deliver.

## Cortex and deep learning (NeuroAI)

NeuroAI is the bidirectional program of using deep nets as quantitative, falsifiable models of cortex (chiefly the primate ventral visual stream) and, conversely, importing cortical structure as inductive bias for AI. The field's empirical backbone is that task-optimized deep nets are, to date, the best predictors of neural responses in V4/IT and of primate object-recognition behavior (Yamins–DiCarlo lineage, benchmarked by Brain-Score), while parallel threads debate whether the brain approximates backpropagation, whether predictive coding is the cortical learning rule, and whether progress needs more biological structure or just more scale. This bears directly on a "grow a Thousand-Brains architecture via indirect encoding" thesis, which sits on the structured/biologically-grounded side of that debate.

**What is established.**

- **Task-optimized DNNs are the leading models of the ventral stream.** Performance-optimized hierarchical CNNs predict single-unit and population responses in V4 and IT better than prior hand-designed models, with a striking correspondence: better ImageNet performance → better neural predictivity, and network depth maps onto the cortical hierarchy (Yamins et al. 2014 PNAS; Yamins & DiCarlo 2016 Nat Neuro; Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte 2014).
- **"Goal-driven" modeling is the dominant methodology.** Fix an ethologically relevant objective, optimize an architecture, then compare fixed layers to neural data — decoupling the three deep-learning design axes (architecture, objective/loss, learning rule) that Richards et al. (2019, Nat Neuro) argue should structure computational neuroscience.
- **Benchmarking is community-scaled.** Brain-Score (Schrimpf et al. 2018 bioRxiv; 2020 Neuron) aggregates 100+ neural/behavioral benchmarks; it revealed diminishing/negative returns of raw ImageNet accuracy on brain-alignment past a point, motivating architectural and training-data interventions rather than pure scaling.
- **Recurrence matters.** Feedforward depth alone fails on "challenging" images that humans/primates solve; recurrent (and top-down/PFC) processing is causally implicated in core object recognition (Kar et al. 2019 Nat Neuro; Kubilius et al. CORnet 2019).
- **Topography can be grown from a spatial cost.** Adding a wiring-length/spatial-smoothness constraint to a task-optimized net (TDANN) reproduces V1 pinwheels and VTC category clusters (face/place patches) — functional cortical maps emerge from task + spatial inductive bias (Margalit et al. 2024 Neuron).
- **The canonical cortical microcircuit is a real, conserved 6-layer motif** (Douglas & Martin), and predictive-coding accounts map its laminar connectivity onto error/prediction computations (Bastos et al. 2012 Neuron; Rao & Ballard 1999 Nat Neuro).
- **Predictive coding can approximate backprop.** Under specific conditions PC with local Hebbian plasticity approximates backprop-of-error along arbitrary computation graphs (Whittington & Bogacz 2017; Millidge, Tschantz & Buckley 2020), one of several "biologically plausible backprop" routes (alongside feedback alignment, target propagation, equilibrium propagation).
- **Backprop-in-the-brain is now a serious hypothesis, not a dismissal.** Lillicrap et al. (2020, Nat Rev Neuro) argue feedback connections could induce activity differences that locally encode backprop-like error signals (the "NGRAD"/neural-gradient-representation hypothesis).
- **Known failure modes of DNNs-as-vision-models:** reliance on local texture/shape over global shape, adversarial/single-pixel shortcuts, poor combinatorial generalization, non-human error patterns (Bowers et al. 2023 BBS; Geirhos et al. shortcut learning).

**Open questions / controversies.**

- **Structure vs scale:** Does brain-alignment and robustness come from more biologically grounded architecture/inductive biases (recurrence, topography, columns, sensorimotor loops) or does scaling data/parameters eventually subsume it? Brain-Score's accuracy-saturation and Bowers-style failures fuel the "structure" side; the scaling successes of large vision/language models fuel the other.
- **Which learning rule?** Backprop-approximation (NGRAD, feedback alignment, target prop, equilibrium prop) vs predictive coding vs purely local Hebbian/self-supervised objectives — no consensus on what cortex actually does, and PC-approximates-backprop results depend on assumptions rarely met in cortex.
- **How much does recurrence/feedback change the story**, and can feedforward nets with more depth "fake" it? (Kar et al. vs shallow-recurrent CORnet trade-offs.)
- **Are behavioral/representational matches evidence of mechanism or just of shared task statistics?** (Identifiability: many nets fit neural data; degeneracy of the model class.)
- **What is the right benchmark?** Static image predictivity vs the embodied/sensorimotor "NeuroAI Turing test" (Zador et al.); brain-model evaluations may need harder tests.
- **Is the canonical microcircuit genuinely canonical/computationally uniform**, or does laminar circuitry vary enough that a single Mountcastle-style module is an over-idealization?
- **Does topography (TDANN) buy function or is it epiphenomenal?** Whether the spatial cost improves task/robustness or merely reproduces maps is unsettled.

**Bearing on the Thousand Brains thesis.**

This literature is the scientific backdrop against which a "grow a Thousand-Brains cortical architecture via indirect encoding" thesis must be positioned, and it supplies both allies and adversaries:

1. **It legitimizes the structured-architecture bet.** The Bowers critique, the Brain-Score accuracy-saturation finding, the causal necessity of recurrence (Kar 2019), and the NeuroAI/embodied-Turing-test agenda (Zador et al. 2023) collectively argue that pure feedforward scaling under-delivers on brain-like, robust, sample-efficient intelligence — which is exactly the TBP/Hawkins premise (columns as repeated learning modules with reference frames, sensorimotor learning, voting). Cite these as the empirical warrant for building structure in rather than scaling up.

2. **TDANN (Margalit et al. 2024) is the strongest existence proof for the core mechanism of the thesis.** It shows a cortical-scale functional organization (maps, columns) can be *grown* from a task objective plus a spatial/geometric cost. That is conceptually a hair's breadth from indirect encoding: replace/augment the spatial-smoothness regularizer with a generative geometric encoding (CPPN/HyperNEAT-style, per Stanley) that produces repeated column motifs and their reference-frame geometry from a compact genome — the "grow via indirect encoding" move. Indirect encoding's selling point (compact genome → large regular, symmetric, repeated-with-variation connectivity that exploits problem geometry) is a near-perfect match to Mountcastle's repeated-column hypothesis.

3. **The three-axis framework (Richards et al. 2019) gives the thesis its vocabulary:** an indirect encoding is a claim about the *architecture* axis (how connectivity is generated), which must be paired with an *objective* (sensorimotor prediction / reference-frame modeling as in Monty) and a *learning rule* (the TBP uses fast Hebbian-like association; the backprop-vs-PC debate tells you which rules are defensible to bolt on).

4. **Caveats to preempt reviewers:** (a) the DiCarlo/Yamins ventral-stream evidence is feedforward-and-static and vision-only, whereas TBP is sensorimotor and modality-general — do not overclaim ventral-stream predictivity as support for a columnar sensorimotor system; frame TBP as addressing the *embodied* gap those benchmarks miss. (b) The "is the canonical microcircuit truly uniform" open question is a direct threat to a single repeated-module genome; acknowledge it. (c) Indirect encoding historically struggled to scale/train to modern-DNN performance (generative neuroevolution results are modest) — the thesis must show the encoding produces *trainable*, competitive modules, not just pretty regular connectivity.
